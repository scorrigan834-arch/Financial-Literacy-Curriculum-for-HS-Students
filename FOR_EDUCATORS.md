# For Educators

## Overview

The Financial Literacy Curriculum is an open-source, decision-based learning program designed for high school students.

Rather than focusing solely on financial definitions and terminology, the curriculum emphasizes real-world decision-making through simulations, activities, and long-term consequence modeling.

Students learn how today's choices can impact future financial outcomes.

---

## Who This Curriculum Is For

Designed for:

- High school students (grades 9–12)
- Personal finance courses
- Career readiness programs
- Business education classes
- Advisory and homeroom programs
- School counseling initiatives

---

## What Makes This Different

Most financial literacy programs teach financial concepts.

This curriculum teaches financial decision-making.

Students explore questions such as:

- Should I borrow money for college?
- How much debt is too much?
- How do career choices affect financial outcomes?
- What are the tradeoffs between spending and investing?
- How can small decisions compound over time?

---

## Curriculum Structure

### Module 1: Money Mindset
Understanding wealth, behavior, and long-term thinking.

### Module 2: Budgeting & Cash Flow
Managing income, expenses, and financial stability.

### Module 3: Banking
Understanding checking, savings, interest, and fraud prevention.

### Module 4: Credit & Debt
Learning how borrowing decisions impact future options.

### Module 5: College & Student Loans
Evaluating educational investments and return on investment.

### Module 6: Career & Income
Exploring compensation, benefits, and career pathways.

### Module 7: Investing
Building long-term wealth through investing principles.

### Module 8: Taxes
Understanding paychecks, tax forms, and tax basics.

### Module 9: Housing & Major Purchases
Evaluating housing and major spending decisions.

### Module 10: Insurance & Risk
Managing risk and protecting financial well-being.

### Module 11: Wealth Building
Creating long-term financial independence strategies.

## Flexible Implementation

Educators may:

### Teach a Single Lesson

Use an individual lesson as a classroom activity.

### Teach a Full Module

Use one module as a standalone unit.

### Implement the Full Curriculum

Use the entire curriculum as a semester-long or year-long program.
